Design System
Insights Builder is an extension of Foursquare Studio that uses maps and datasets to create custom dashboards. Beginning as an internal tool, this design effort optimized engineering workflows, tested early-stage concepts for future product planning, and informed the next stages of our design system.

Creating a Minimal Viable Product
Insights Builder was originally created as an internal engineering proof-of-concept, which they used to build custom dashboards to sell to existing customers. Potential customers expressed interest in this as a self-serve tool, IADB (Inter-American Development Bank) agreeing to work with us to test this concept's product potential.
Defining Scope
Synthesizing Existing Customer Conversations
By the time I had joined this project, our sales team had numerous conversations with IADB and other potential customers. I asked for meeting recordings to source overlapping themes and discover which topics required clarification from the customer or internal aligment with engineering or marketing.
Setting and Scoping Requirements
The customer-call synthesis matrix formed the foundation for user-stories that my product manager and I co-wrote. My product manager broke these stories out into phases, scoping our 'alpha phase' for the minimal viable product. We then grouped user-stories into tasks and collaborated with our engineering lead to prioritize items and create tickets in productboard.
Setting Design Guidelines
I met with my director to establish workflow priorities that ensured this work would also contribute to design unification and cross-product efforts within Foursquare at large.

Competitive Analysis
For specific tasks, like layout, we established design principles that reinforce user needs and see how other products’ design strategies align or depart from them. This helped inform where design effort should be spent on these tasks for the highest level of impact.
Grid System and Chart Sizing
Guidelines around responsive behavior, chart placement and sizing prevented chart UIs from breaking, reduced customization effort without compromising flexibilty, and minimized the number of edge-cases necessary to design for. I learned from my engineers about what libraries they use, such as the React grid and Nivo charts, to align on what parameters could be set. For our charts I audited all the data-visualization offerings we provide and assigned them to appropriate size restraints by grid unit, informing the simplest grid-system to use.
Chart Design & Specs
To ensure consistency amongst the chart designs, I ran a workshop with other product designers to align on the chart's sub-elements' placements and styles. This enabled us to create a datavis component as an adaptive figma asset to ease future design efforts.
Task Simplification
Editing a Chart
Core user flows, such as editing or adding charts to the dashboard, were reworked to eliminate any redundant interactions or unecessary feature capabilities. Features that remained were reprioritized and partitioned into basic and advanced experiences in order to accomodate two distinctly different user preferences from our initial customer research.
Automating Processes
When possible, I sought to automate processes by using underlying assets or metadata (datasets, data columns, filters, color palettes, and names) used in the source map. The goal was to provide users with a 'best-guess' while making it as convenient as possible to edit the result to their liking.
Highlighting Design System Gaps
Changes to the Studio Mapping UI
Showing the case map to administrators, they were primarily interested in cases that clustered on main campus, as students living off campus were out of their jurisdictional control. With this knowledge, we decided to explore a spatial visualization at this smaller scale.
Cupcake Dark Mode
At CMU, students are grouped into one or more "mavilies," which include living clusters, in-person classes, sports teams, and performing arts groups. Administrators find the mavilies most useful as they often point to sources of localized outbreaks and can be used to measure students' levels of probable interaction. These views allow admin to see if there are positive case trends within dorm floors and living mavilies, and investigate whether or not these patterns are related to other non-living mavilies.

Impact
